Puppy

Taking a puppy into your home is a big decision that comes with a lot of responsibility. From the initial research on finding a suitable breed to the training and exercise of your puppy: every step contributes to a healthy and happy dog.

Choosing: Which Breed Suits You?

Before you acquire a puppy, it is important to consider your lifestyle and how it might change in the coming years. Different dog breeds have varying needs and characteristics. Think about energy levels, grooming, how well they get along with children, and even how much they shed. Do thorough research and choose a breed that fits your situation, so that you select a dog you can care for well and keep happy. Still undecided? Then take the test on the website for advice on which breed is best for you.

Finding the Right Breeder

Have you found a suitable breed? The next step is to look for a reliable breeder. An ethical breeder pays attention to the health and temperament of the parent dogs, socializes the puppies well, and ensures they are raised in a clean, safe environment. Legally, puppies may leave the nest at seven weeks, but eight weeks is more common. They must be microchipped and vaccinated before leaving. Be critical: a good breeder asks about your home situation and sometimes even helps in choosing the puppy that suits you best.

Bringing the Puppy Home: Preparation and the First Weeks

A puppy is curious and explores its surroundings even with its teeth. Therefore, make sure your home is puppy-proof: keep valuable items out of reach and check that houseplants are not toxic. Get all the essentials, such as a crate, food bowls, a comfortable sleeping spot, toys, and suitable food.

The first days in a new home can be exciting for the puppy. Give it time to adjust and establish a routine. Puppies sleep 16 to 20 hours a day, so ensure it gets enough rest. Socialization is essential during this period: introduce new sounds, people, and animals in a calm and positive way. Ignoring startle responses helps the puppy to become less anxious.

Training and Exercise: The Foundation for a Healthy Puppy

A puppy learns the most between 8 and 12 weeks. This is the perfect time to playfully teach basic commands and get it accustomed to different situations. A puppy training course is ideal for teaching behavior and addressing questions about training. Additionally, your puppy learns to interact with other dogs of the same age.

Exercise is important, but it should not be too strenuous. The joints and bones of a puppy are still developing, and overexertion can lead to growth problems. Short play sessions and calm walks are ideal. When a puppy suddenly lies down or refuses to continue walking, it is indicating that it needs rest. Listen carefully and do not force exercise.

With proper preparation, training, and care, your puppy grows into a healthy and balanced dog. By focusing on socialization, training, and exercise, you lay a strong foundation for a lifelong bond!

Puberty

Puberty in dogs usually begins around 6 months and can continue until about 2 years, depending on the breed. During this period, your dog undergoes both physical and mental changes. Although they reach sexual maturity, they are still developing mentally. This can lead to behavioral changes that may be challenging for some owners.

Hormonal Changes and Behavioral Change

Due to the hormonal changes during this period, your dog will behave differently. Although they become sexually mature, they are not yet fully mentally developed. This means your dog will test its limits. As a result, it may listen less, be less obedient, and sometimes exhibit unwanted behavior. It is important to remain patient, as this phase is part of its normal development.

Fear Phase and Behavioral Changes

During puberty, your dog may experience a second fear phase, in which it reacts fearfully to new or unfamiliar situations. This can manifest as barking, withdrawing, or refusing to continue walking. It is essential not to ignore this phase, but to respond with calmness and consistency. Avoid comforting or rewarding fearful behavior, as this might worsen the problem.

Training and Patience

Puberty is a phase that can cause frustration for the owner, but fortunately it eventually passes! During this time, your dog will continue to develop both physically and mentally. This may be accompanied by behavioral changes such as decreased obedience, testing boundaries, and even unwanted behavior. It is important to remain patient and train consistently, despite the challenges that may arise.

Instead of reacting with frustration, it is crucial to remain calm and guide your dog with positive reinforcement. Use this period to review basic commands and allow your dog to practice in various situations, including with other dogs or distractions. Building a strong bond of trust will help support your dog during this transition.

Although it can be difficult at times, this developmental phase is only a temporary challenge. Stay calm and consistent, and with patience your dog will eventually outgrow puberty and mature into the adult phase, where it is more calm and balanced.

Adults

When a dog reaches the adult phase, it is physically and socially developed, but it continues learning throughout its life. Depending on the breed, a dog becomes fully mature around its first to second year. In this phase, it is important to closely monitor its health, provide sufficient exercise, and challenge it both mentally and physically.

Health and Preventive Care

An adult dog is generally strong and energetic, but preventive care remains essential. Regular veterinary check-ups help detect health problems early. Parasites, such as worms, fleas, and ticks, can be prevented with preventive treatments such as deworming and flea remedies. Good hygiene is also important: wounds need to be kept clean to prevent bacterial infections.

Dental care is often a neglected aspect of a dog's health. By regularly brushing its teeth or using special dental sticks, plaque can be reduced and dental problems can be prevented. A healthy set of teeth contributes to the overall well‑being of the dog and prevents painful conditions later in life. Additionally, it is important to pay attention to the nutritional values of the food, avoid too much salt to prevent kidney issues, and provide sports feeding for active and energetic dogs.

An Adult Dog Continues to Learn

Although a dog is an adult, its learning process never stops. New commands, challenges, and socialization remain important to keep it mentally sharp and socially adept. Training can help correct unwanted behavior and teach the dog new skills.

Exercise and Mental Challenge

Adult dogs need regular exercise to stay fit, healthy, and mentally stimulated. The required amount of exercise varies depending on the breed and the dog's energy level. In addition to daily walks, dogs can participate in dog sports such as agility, flyball, or canicross. These activities not only help burn excess energy, but also strengthen the bond between dog and owner.

Dog sports offer many benefits, but it is important not to overlook the risks. Intensive sports can cause injuries, especially if a dog is not properly trained or warmed up. Therefore, it is essential to gradually increase the workload and ensure that the dog is physically suited for the chosen sport. It is important to note that, depending on the breed and the dog's health, certain sports or activities may be more suitable than others.

Senior

When a dog reaches senior age, its body and behavior change. This requires adjustments in care and attention for potential health issues. Dogs are generally considered seniors from around seven years of age, depending on the breed. Larger breeds reach this phase earlier than smaller dogs, due to their shorter lifespan. During this period, endurance decreases, metabolism slows down, and activity level may decline. This can lead to changes in weight and other signs of aging.

Care and Adjustments

Dogs age and sometimes experience complaints. These are some common points to make life better for your loyal companion.

  • Adapted Exercise: Long, intensive walks are less suitable. Opt for shorter, calmer walks and provide a soft, comfortable resting place.
  • Home Comfort: Provide a warm, soft resting place and avoid slippery floors where your dog could slip. An orthopedic cushion can alleviate joint discomfort.
  • Appropriate Nutrition: A dog's dental health may decline, which can cause pain when eating. Consider alternative food such as soft meat or softened kibble.
  • Regular Health Check-Ups: Seniors have an increased risk of conditions such as arthritis, diabetes, heart issues, and dental problems. Early detection and treatment can reduce discomfort and pain, and prolong the lifespan.

Saying Goodbye: The Final Care for Your Dog

Unfortunately, saying goodbye is part of life with a dog. At some point, a dog may suffer from life-limiting issues, such as severe pain, difficulty standing up, constant panting, incontinence, or loss of appetite. As an owner, you often notice when the quality of life decreases. A veterinarian can provide advice on the health condition and possible treatment options.

Euthanasia

When a dog is suffering and no improvement is possible, euthanasia can be a humane choice. Some veterinary practices face staff shortages, which can make it difficult to secure an appointment quickly. It is therefore advisable to discuss this with a veterinarian in a timely manner. There are also veterinarians and private professionals who offer at-home euthanasia, so the dog can remain in a familiar environment.

Burial or Cremation

After saying goodbye, you may choose either burial or cremation. In some municipalities, a dog may be buried in the garden, provided the proper depth is maintained. Check local regulations for this. Another option is cremation, where you can scatter the ashes or keep them in an urn. Today, there is also an environmentally friendly water cremation, a CO₂-neutral option.

Mourning and Coping

Everyone says goodbye to a pet in their own way. Other pets in the household can also be affected by the loss and may exhibit changes in behavior. It is important to give them extra attention and rest. Grieving takes time, and it may help to give memories of your dog a special place, for example with a photo or a memorial space in your home.
